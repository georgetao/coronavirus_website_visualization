Countries at Risk

Countries with Highest Number of Cases Timelapse

In the video above, (made in Tableau) we can see a timelapse of new cases by country. As it first peaked in China, many other countries have been severely impacted such as the United States, Italy, and Spain, with the US leading the total number of confirmed cases at over 350K as of April 7th. It's important to understand the severeity of this pandemic. Through the timelapse, we can see how quickly a few cases can turn into thousands of cases in a matter of days. However, to truly understand the impact the novel coronavirus has, we want to take a deeper dive into a few other metrics. Oftentimes, bigger is better, but causes smaller countries to fall out of the visualization. We intend to correct this below.

Comparing Growth Rates of Countries on April 7th

The above visualization shows the number of new cases on April 7th compared to the total confirmed cases of a country. The trend line represents a general path most countries will follow as they grow in cases. This is a snapshot of this growth on April 7th. What we can learn from this visualization is that the countries to the right of the trendline, (China, Italy, South Korea) have moved away from the trendline as their growth rates decrease. In a infectious trajectory curve, they are on the downward sloping trend. They must still be careful before restarting "normal" life, as resurgences of the virus can occur, but these countries are out of the eye of the storm.

Top 10 Countries at Risk

This visualization shows the distributions of mortality (in light orange) to total confirmed cases (in dark blue). It sorts the countries by percent infected rather than confirmed cases from left to right. Hover over each country to see additional stats regarding each one. What we can learn from this visualization is that even though the number of confirmed cases are very high for the US, the overall percent of the population infection is minimal. Looking at Italy, Switzerland, and Spain, there are less confirmed cases but a high percent of the population is impacted. Size is relative as different countries have different population sizes. This can paint a better picture as to which countries are the most heavily affected as the higher the infected rate is, the higher the strain on the country's healthcare system.

Percent Infected as of April 7th

This visualization provides a distribution of countries based on the number of confirmed cases and the percent infected rate. It would be foolish to say that we should only look at percent infected or vice versa, but this provides a clearer picture of the impact it has on any one country. Countries that have higher percent infected need stricter measures to flatten the curve as these countries' healthcare systems are overwhelmed by the number of patients and the supply strain. Similarly, countries with high total confirmed cases can also overwhelm health care systems even though the sentiment of the extent virus to the general public may be very limited.
